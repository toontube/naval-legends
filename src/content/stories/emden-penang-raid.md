Before dawn on October 28, 1914, the German light cruiser SMS Emden entered Penang harbor flying no flag and carrying a dummy fourth funnel made of canvas and wood. British warships had four funnels. Emden had three. In the dark, the silhouette passed.

Emden had been terrorizing Allied shipping across the Indian Ocean for two months. Under Captain Karl von Müller, she'd captured or sunk 25 merchant ships, shelled the oil tanks at Madras, and tied up dozens of Allied warships trying to find her. The British called her the "Swan of the East" because Müller followed the rules of war, always letting crews evacuate before sinking their ships.

Penang was supposed to be safe. The harbor held a Russian cruiser, Zhemchug, and a French destroyer, Mousquet, among other vessels. Zhemchug's captain, Baron Cherkasov, was not aboard his ship. He was at the Eastern & Oriental Hotel, reportedly in the company of a woman.

Emden closed to 300 yards and fired a torpedo into Zhemchug's hull. Then another. The Russian cruiser exploded and sank in minutes. 82 Russian sailors died.

On the way out of the harbor, Emden encountered the French destroyer Mousquet, which tried to fight. Emden sank her too. Müller stopped to pick up 36 French survivors from the water before leaving.

Cherkasov watched his ship go down from the hotel. He was court-martialed. The tribunal found him guilty of negligence. His ship had been at anchor with torpedo nets not deployed, watertight doors open, and most of the crew asleep. He got three and a half years in prison.

Emden's run ended three weeks later. On November 9, 1914, the Australian cruiser HMAS Sydney found her at Direction Island in the Cocos Islands, where she'd stopped to destroy a British cable and wireless station. Sydney outgunned her badly. After a brutal fight, Müller ran Emden aground on North Keeling Island to save his remaining crew.

Müller became a prisoner of war but was treated with respect by the British, who admired his conduct. He was awarded the Iron Cross, First Class. After the war, he returned to Germany and died in 1923.

The wreck of Zhemchug is still in Penang harbor. The Eastern & Oriental Hotel is still open.
