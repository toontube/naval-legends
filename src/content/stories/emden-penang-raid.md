On the morning of October 28, 1914, the Russian protected cruiser Zhemchug sat at anchor in Penang harbor. Her boilers were cold. Her ammunition hoists had no power. The keys to her magazine were ashore. No lookouts were posted.

Her captain, Commander Baron Cherkassov, was at the Eastern & Oriental Hotel with a woman.

At 05:18, a torpedo hit the Zhemchug aft of her second funnel. Ten minutes later, a second torpedo broke the ship in half. Cherkassov watched from the hotel as his cruiser rolled over and sank in the Straits of Malacca. Eighty-two of his crew were dead.

The ship that killed them was SMS Emden, a German light cruiser that had spent two months terrorizing the Indian Ocean. And she had sailed straight into a British-controlled harbor, in plain sight, because nobody thought to question the extra smokestack.

## The fake funnel

SMS Emden had three funnels. Most British cruisers in the region had two or four. A three-funnel warship entering Penang at 04:30 in the morning should have raised questions. Captain Karl von Muller made sure it didn't.

Before the raid, Emden's crew rigged a dummy fourth funnel from canvas and scrap metal. In the dark, from a distance, she looked like HMS Yarmouth, a British Town-class light cruiser with four stacks. Harbor lookouts saw four funnels and waved her through.

Von Muller brought the Emden within 300 yards of the Zhemchug before running up the Imperial German naval ensign. By then it was too late to do anything about it.

## A ship that couldn't fight back

The Zhemchug was an Izumrud-class protected cruiser, 3,050 tons, armed with eight 120mm guns and five torpedo tubes. On paper, she could have put up a fight. On the morning of October 28, she was a floating target.

She had arrived in Penang two days earlier for boiler repairs. Only one of her sixteen Yarrow boilers was operational. That meant she couldn't move and her ammunition hoists had no steam to lift shells from the magazines below. The crew had stacked twelve ready rounds on deck. That was it.

Commander Cherkassov had given most of the crew shore leave. The men still aboard were not standing watch. The British commander of the China Station, Vice Admiral Martyn Jerram, had warned the Russians to keep their ships ready. Cherkassov ignored the warning.

He left the ship that evening and went to the Eastern & Oriental Hotel, the grandest building in George Town. The Sarkies brothers had built it in 1885 to cater to wealthy travelers arriving through the Suez Canal. By 1914, it was the social center of colonial Penang, with a sea-facing promenade and views across the Straits of Malacca. The same straits where, eight hours later, his crew would be dying.

## "Swan of the East"

The ship that killed the Zhemchug had been busy.

SMS Emden was a Dresden-class light cruiser, 3,664 tons, ten 10.5cm guns. When war broke out in August 1914, she was stationed with the German East Asia Squadron in Tsingtao, China. While the rest of the squadron sailed east across the Pacific under Admiral von Spee, Captain von Muller convinced his commander to let the Emden operate alone in the Indian Ocean.

It was one of the best decisions of the war, at least for Germany. Over two months, the Emden captured or sank 25 merchant ships. She shelled the oil storage tanks at Madras, starting fires visible for miles. She cut the cable station at Diego Garcia. Insurance rates for Allied shipping in the Indian Ocean shot up. The value of exports in the Bay of Bengal dropped 61 percent in her first month of operations.

The British, French, Japanese, and Russian navies had over seventy warships hunting for her. Von Muller kept slipping away by changing course unpredictably and using captured coal ships to refuel at sea.

The crew called her "Schwan des Ostens." The Swan of the East.

## Ten minutes in Penang

At 04:30 on October 28, the Emden approached Penang from the north with her fake fourth funnel erected and all lights darkened. She entered the harbor channel at 18 knots.

The French patrol boats on guard duty saw four funnels and assumed she was HMS Yarmouth returning from patrol. They let her pass.

At 05:18, still in the dark, von Muller ordered the dummy funnel struck and the German ensign raised. From 400 yards, the Emden fired a torpedo into the Zhemchug's starboard side. It hit aft of the second funnel.

The Russian crew scrambled to respond. A few men managed to fire back with the twelve rounds stacked on deck. Without steam for the ammunition hoists, that was all they had.

The Emden circled. At 05:28, she fired a second torpedo from her port side at 800 yards. This one hit near the Zhemchug's bridge and detonated in the forward magazine. The explosion broke the cruiser in half. She went down in minutes.

Eighty-two Russian sailors died. A hundred and forty-three were wounded. Malay fishermen in small boats pulled survivors from the water.

The Emden then encountered the French destroyer Mousquet returning to harbor. She sank her too, picking up 36 French survivors before heading back out to sea. The whole attack lasted less than an hour.

## The court-martial

Commander Cherkassov survived, of course. He survived from the comfort of the E&O Hotel, watching through windows that faced the same water where his crew was drowning.

The Russian Navy charged him with gross negligence. At his court-martial in Vladivostok, the list of failures was long: no lookouts posted, magazine keys ashore, only one boiler operational, most of the crew on leave, no attempt to follow Vice Admiral Jerram's warnings. His deputy, Lieutenant Kulibin, was charged alongside him.

Cherkassov was sentenced to three and a half years in prison, stripped of his rank, and expelled from the Russian Navy. Kulibin got eighteen months. The sentences were lenient by wartime standards. Eighty-two families probably disagreed.

## What happened to the Emden

The Emden's luck ran out twelve days later.

On November 9, 1914, von Muller brought her to the Cocos Islands to destroy the British cable and wireless station on Direction Island. He sent a landing party of 50 men ashore under Lieutenant Hellmuth von Mucke to smash the equipment.

While the landing party was on the island, the Australian cruiser HMAS Sydney appeared. The Sydney outgunned the Emden and the fight was short. Von Muller beached his wrecked ship on North Keeling Island. He lost 134 men killed and 69 wounded.

But the 50 men on Direction Island were stranded. What von Mucke did next deserves its own article. He commandeered a rotting 97-ton schooner called the Ayesha and sailed it to the Dutch East Indies. From there, his men traveled by steamer to Arabia, fought Bedouin raiders, crossed the desert, met the Emir of Mecca, and eventually reached the Ottoman railway. They arrived in Constantinople in May 1915 and made it back to Germany in June. All 50 survived. They were the only German military unit to return home from overseas during the entire war.

## The memorial in Penang

There's a memorial to the Zhemchug's dead at Western Road Cemetery in George Town. It was erected in 1975 and lists 82 names.

The Eastern & Oriental Hotel is still open. It had a renovation in the 2000s and again for its 140th anniversary in 2025. You can book a sea-facing suite and look out at the same water where the Zhemchug went down.

The Emden's wreck sat on North Keeling Island for years, rusting in the surf. Pieces of her were salvaged as souvenirs. The Japanese dismantled most of what was left during World War II.

The fake funnel, the one that let a German cruiser sail past Allied patrols and into a harbor full of warships, was canvas stretched over a metal frame. It probably took the crew an afternoon to build. It worked once, and that was enough.
